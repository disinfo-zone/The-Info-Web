The Californian Ideology is a 1995 essay by the British media theorists Richard Barbrook and Andy Cameron of the Hypermedia Research Centre at the University of Westminster in London, which diagnosed the worldview emerging from 1990s San Francisco Bay Area technology culture as a contradictory fusion of hippie counterculture and free-market neoliberalism held together by a faith in technological determinism. The authors described it as "a bizarre fusion of the cultural bohemianism of San Francisco with the hi-tech industries of Silicon Valley," and as "cybernetic libertarianism," a "mish-mash of hippie anarchism and economic liberalism beefed up with lots of technological determinism."[^1][^2]

### The Essay and Its Argument

Barbrook and Cameron wrote the essay in 1994 and 1995 and first published it in *Mute* magazine, number 3, in 1995; it circulated widely on the nettime internet mailing list and appeared in a definitive version in the journal *Science as Culture* (volume 6, number 1, 1996). They argued that a loose alliance of West Coast "writers, hackers, capitalists and artists" had defined a "heterogeneous orthodoxy for the coming information age" that "promiscuously combines the free-wheeling spirit of the hippies and the entrepreneurial zeal of the yuppies." The new faith reconciled the radical individualism of the 1960s with the market individualism of the 1980s by treating the spread of networked computing as an autonomous, liberating force.[^1][^2]

Central to the critique was the claim that the ideology rested on a "nearly universal belief in technological determinism," a faith that allowed its adherents to hold incompatible visions at once: an "electronic agora" of free expression and digital democracy on one side, and an "electronic marketplace" of deregulated commerce on the other, without resolving the contradiction. The authors named *Wired* magazine, launched in 1993, as "the monthly bible of the virtual class," and noted that it reproduced the right-wing futurism of Alvin and Heidi Toffler and the libertarian politics associated with Newt Gingrich and his Progress and Freedom Foundation while presenting itself as countercultural.[^2][^3]

### The Virtual Class, Jefferson, and the Erasure of the State

Barbrook and Cameron borrowed the term "virtual class" to describe the "techno-intelligentsia of cognitive scientists, engineers, computer scientists, video-game developers and all the other communications specialists" who produced the new media, a stratum caught between market reward and the insecurity of contract employment, and so suspended between hippie autonomy and corporate discipline. The ideology, they argued, promised that digital technology would deliver a renewed "Jeffersonian democracy" in which individuals expressed themselves freely in cyberspace, a self-image the authors attacked by pointing out that Thomas Jefferson was a slaveholder who owned roughly two hundred people, and that the Californian utopia ignored the racial underclass on which Silicon Valley's prosperity depended.[^2][^3]

A further charge was historical amnesia about the role of the state. The authors stressed that the very networks the ideology celebrated as products of free enterprise, above all the ARPANET that became the internet, had been built with massive public funding from the U.S. military and government, a fact the anti-statist gospel of "cybernetic libertarianism" suppressed. They located the worldview as a convergence of the New Left and the New Right, in which the countercultural rejection of the state from the left met the market fundamentalist rejection of the state from the right, producing a politics that mistook a publicly subsidized infrastructure for a triumph of laissez-faire.[^1][^2]

### Reception and Afterlife

The essay became one of the most cited critiques of 1990s digital culture. The critic Andrew Leonard of *Salon* called it "one of the most penetrating critiques of neo-conservative digital hypesterism yet published," while Louis Rossetto, the founder and editor of *Wired*, wrote an angry rebuttal accusing the authors of "profound ignorance of economics." Commentators have since argued that Barbrook and Cameron anticipated the later contradictions of the platform economy and the libertarian politics of the technology elite decades before they became mainstream concerns.[^1][^4]

In the decades after publication, observers traced the fusion the essay named into the politics of the Bay Area technology industry, including the libertarian and anti-statist commitments associated with [[Peter Thiel]] and the broader transhumanist and longtermist culture, and revisited the essay on its thirtieth anniversary as a still-operative description of Silicon Valley's self-understanding. Barbrook returned to the theme in his book *Imaginary Futures: From Thinking Machines to the Global Village* (Pluto Press, 2007), extending the analysis of how Cold War American technology was reimagined as a tool of inevitable progress.[^4][^5]
