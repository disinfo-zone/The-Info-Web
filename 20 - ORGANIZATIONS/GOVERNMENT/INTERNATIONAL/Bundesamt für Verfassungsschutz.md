The Bundesamt für Verfassungsschutz (BfV, Federal Office for the Protection of the Constitution) is the domestic intelligence and counterintelligence service of the Federal Republic of [[West Germany]], later unified [[Germany]]. Established on September 7, 1950, it operates under the authority of the Federal Interior Ministry and is legally distinct from the [[Bundesnachrichtendienst]] (BND), which handles foreign intelligence. The BfV is responsible for identifying and monitoring domestic threats to the constitutional order including espionage, terrorism, and extremist movements of both left and right.[^1]

### Structure and Mandate

The BfV lacks arrest powers - it is an intelligence service, not a law enforcement agency - and operates in parallel with state-level Landesämter für Verfassungsschutz (LfV), which have equivalent mandates within their respective German states. The division between federal and state jurisdiction in domestic intelligence has been a persistent organizational challenge, particularly in cases involving networks that span state boundaries.

During the [[Cold War]] the BfV's primary counterintelligence mission was identifying and neutralizing penetrations of [[West Germany|West German]] government and political institutions by the [[Stasi]] and other Eastern bloc services. The scale of this task was substantial: the Stasi's foreign intelligence directorate, the [[Hauptverwaltung Aufklärung]] (HVA) under [[Markus Wolf]], placed long-term agents throughout West German political parties, ministries, intelligence services, and private institutions.[^1]

### The Guillaume Case

The BfV's most consequential Cold War operation was the investigation that led to the arrest of [[Günter Guillaume]] in April 1974. The BfV had been monitoring Guillaume on the basis of signals intelligence indicating his connection to East German intelligence since at least 1973. Despite this, it took several more months before it informed Chancellor [[Willy Brandt]] of the evidence, and Brandt continued to use Guillaume in his work while the investigation was completed.

Guillaume was arrested on April 24, 1974. His arrest, and his defiant declaration of his identity as an East German officer at the moment of arrest, triggered a political crisis that ended with Brandt's resignation on May 7, 1974. The handling of the investigation - particularly the delay in informing Brandt and the decision to allow Guillaume continued access while the investigation concluded - was subsequently criticized as having prolonged the penetration unnecessarily.[^1]

### Stasi Penetration

The BfV was itself penetrated by Stasi agents during the Cold War. The HVA succeeded in placing sources within the BfV, providing East German intelligence with information about ongoing counterintelligence investigations. The full scope of BfV penetration by the Stasi was assessed only partially after reunification, when surviving Stasi archives were opened to researchers and former HVA officers were debriefed.[^1]

### Post-Reunification

Following German reunification in 1990, the BfV's mandate expanded to include monitoring extremist movements within the former East German states, integrating personnel and networks into a unified service, and adapting its counterintelligence mission from the Cold War Soviet-bloc focus to new threat environments including Islamic extremism, far-right and far-left violence, and cyber espionage. The BfV maintains headquarters in Cologne with an increasing presence in Berlin.[^1]
