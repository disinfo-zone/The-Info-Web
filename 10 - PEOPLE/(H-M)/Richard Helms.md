Richard Helms (March 30, 1913 - October 22, 2002) served as Director of Central Intelligence from June 30, 1966, to February 2, 1973. He authorized [[Operation CHAOS]] in August 1967 under direct pressure from President [[Lyndon B. Johnson|Lyndon Johnson]], ordered the destruction of CHAOS and MKULTRA records in 1973 before congressional investigation, and was convicted in 1977 of making false and misleading statements to Congress about CIA involvement in efforts to prevent Salvador Allende from taking power in Chile.[^1]

### Operation CHAOS and Records Destruction

On receiving LBJ's instruction in August 1967 to determine whether foreign powers were directing the American antiwar movement, Helms tasked [[James Jesus Angleton]]'s Counterintelligence Staff with establishing the Special Operations Group that became [[Operation CHAOS]]. He subsequently expanded the program under Nixon's presidency. In 1973, before the Watergate-related congressional investigations reached CHAOS, Helms ordered the destruction of CHAOS operational records. A cache of budget and operational documents survived only because it had been misfiled. In the same period, he ordered the destruction of records related to [[MKULTRA]], the CIA's human experimentation program. The Church Committee found that this records destruction deprived investigators of a complete documentary record of both programs.[^1]

### Chile and Congressional Perjury Conviction

In 1970, Helms received a direct instruction from President Nixon to conduct a covert operation to prevent Allende from assuming the Chilean presidency - what Nixon called making the Chilean economy "scream." In 1973, when Helms testified before the Senate Foreign Relations Committee regarding CIA activities in Chile, he stated that the CIA had not "tried to overthrow the government of Chile" and had not "passed money to the opponents of Allende." The 1974 coup against Allende and subsequent Church Committee investigation revealed that both statements were false. In 1977, Helms was charged with two misdemeanor counts of making false and misleading statements to Congress. He pleaded no contest (nolo contendere) and was fined $2,000 and given a two-year suspended sentence. CIA veterans gave Helms a standing ovation in the courthouse corridor after sentencing.[^1]

### Psychic Research

Helms personally approved the continuation and expansion of the CIA's psychic research program, initially known as the Biofield Measurements Program and later as the Paranormal Perception Research Project. His decision was based in part on concern about perceived Soviet advancements in parapsychology research. He authorized testing of [[Uri Geller]] at [[Stanford Research Institute|SRI]] and the program that evolved into [[Project Stargate]].[^2]
