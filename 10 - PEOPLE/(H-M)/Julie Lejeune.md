Julie Lejeune, an eight-year-old girl from Charleroi, [[Belgium]], disappeared on July 9, 1995, alongside her friend [[Melissa Russo]] while riding bicycles near their neighborhood. The abduction occurred in broad daylight, with the girls lured into a white van driven by [[Marc Dutroux]] and his accomplice [[Michel Lelievre]], who had been scouting potential victims under instructions from [[Jean-Michel Nihoul]]. Lejeune and Russo were immediately imprisoned in a hidden basement dungeon at Dutroux's residence in [[Marcinelle, Belgium]], a concealed cell constructed in 1993 with assistance from handyman [[Claude Thirault]], who later reported Dutroux's explicit plans to kidnap girls for profit. The dungeon featured soundproof walls, a rudimentary ventilation system, and a locked access point disguised as shelving, designed specifically to hold young victims for extended periods. Surveillance by police inspector [[Rene Michaux]]'s [[Operation Othello]] team began on August 10, 1995, but failed to detect the girls despite their presence in the house. On August 22, 1995, Dutroux kidnapped two more teenagers, [[An Marchal]] and [[Eefje Lambrecks]], temporarily housing them upstairs due to the occupied basement, before murdering them elsewhere; police monitoring overlooked these activities entirely.[^1]

### Abduction and Imprisonment

Lejeune endured months of captivity in the Marcinelle basement, subjected to repeated sexual assaults by Dutroux, who documented some encounters on videotape as part of his production of child rape materials for sale on the black market. These tapes, labeled deceptively with mainstream film titles, depicted drugged or sedated victims and were returned to Dutroux's wife [[Michelle Martin]] after a December 1995 search, despite containing evidence of his crimes. On December 13, 1995, during Dutroux's arrest on unrelated auto theft charges involving the kidnapping of three teenagers, a locksmith and detective Michaux heard children's cries emanating from the basement; however, the search overlooked the hidden cells behind the plywood partition, attributing the sounds to external sources. Lejeune and Russo, locked inside, received minimal sustenance from Martin, who occasionally provided food and water but prioritized her own evasion of detection. This neglect led to their gradual starvation over the four months Dutroux remained incarcerated, a period during which he was eliminated as a suspect in their disappearance in January 1996 and released on "humanitarian grounds" in March 1996. Upon his return, Dutroux discovered the girls' emaciated bodies and buried them on a property in Sars-la-Buissière, Belgium, alongside the corpse of his associate [[Bernard Weinstein]], whom he had murdered in November 1995 by drugging and burying alive.[^1]

The basement conditions exacerbated their suffering, with temperatures dropping below freezing in winter and inadequate hygiene facilities forcing reliance on a single bucket for waste. Videotapes seized later revealed Dutroux's methodical approach to conditioning victims, using threats and sedatives to ensure compliance, as corroborated by testimonies from rescued survivors [[Sabine Dardenne]] and [[Laetitia Delhez]]. Lejeune's specific role in Dutroux's operations aligned with Nihoul's demands for young girls to supply to high-profile clients, evidenced by phone records showing over twenty calls between Dutroux and Nihoul in early August 1996, coinciding with the abduction of Delhez. Investigations into Nihoul's company [[Achats Services Commerces]] (ASCO) uncovered white Mercedes vehicles used for scouting schoolgirls, matching witness descriptions of the van involved in Lejeune's kidnapping. Posthumous autopsies confirmed severe malnutrition and mutilations on Lejeune's body, including evidence of prolonged restraint and sexual trauma, contradicting initial police narratives of simple starvation.[^1]

### Discovery and Broader Network Implications

The bodies of Lejeune and Russo were exhumed on August 18, 1996, following Dutroux's arrest for the abduction of Delhez, whose case provided the partial license plate leading to his properties. Forensic analysis revealed the girls had been dead for months, their remains showing signs of postmortem interference consistent with Dutroux's letter to Lejeune's father claiming the kidnapping was for an organized "snuff orgy" involving multiple participants. This correspondence, intercepted during the investigation, implicated a network beyond Dutroux, including Nihoul, who faced charges as an accomplice in Delhez's kidnapping alongside lawyer [[Annie Bouty]] and associate [[Marleen De Cokere]]. Seized materials from Nihoul's residence included hundreds of violent child pornography videos, some featuring identifiable high-society figures, linking to broader trafficking operations. The [[X-Dossier]], compiled by prosecutor [[Michel Bourlet]]'s team under investigating judge [[Jean-Marc Connerotte]], documented connections to cold cases like the murder of [[Christine Van Hees]], suggesting Lejeune's abduction fit a pattern of profit-driven kidnappings for elite clientele. Connerotte's removal in October 1996 after attending a victim support event sparked the White March protests, with up to 300,000 participants decrying perceived cover-ups involving figures like Justice Minister [[Melchior Wathelet]], who authorized Dutroux's 1992 early release despite his prior convictions for raping five girls aged 11-19.[^1]

Lejeune's case fueled scrutiny of police incompetence, including the Othello team's failure to act on Thirault's 1993 tip-off about the dungeon renovations, where Dutroux explicitly discussed kidnapping techniques yielding 150,000 francs per victim. The investigation revealed Dutroux's multiple European bank accounts with large transfers, funding his seven properties despite unemployment, and ties to video duplication services via Weinstein. Nihoul's acquittal on kidnapping charges in 2004, despite jury conviction, and early release in 2005, alongside Dutroux's life sentence, highlighted systemic protections for network members. Witnesses like [[Regina Louf]] (X1) described similar abductions for blackmail parties at venues linked to [[Baron Benoit de Bonvoisin]] and [[Count Leopold Lippens]], with Lejeune's fate echoing mutilations in snuff materials traced to operations like [[Apollo Disks]]. The [[Morkhoven Workgroup]] identified potential matches of Lejeune in seized pornography, connecting to international rings involving [[John Stamford]]'s [[Spartacus Network]] and [[Gerrit-Jan Ulrich]]'s Zandvoort files.[^1]
